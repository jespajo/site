Croquembouche

I

n 1986, my dad quit his job at a restaurant in Sydney, took a taxi to the airport and tried to board a plane to New Zealand. They didn’t let him fly. He’d stayed in Australia longer than his visa allowed, they said, and could only get on a plane that took him home to France. He didn’t want to go home—he was enjoying a would tour, partly inspired by a desire to avoid his home country’s mandatory military service. Unsure what to do, he asked for his job back at the restaurant. The once and future kitchen hand.

A few months later, working there, he met my mum. She was a 21-year-old Australian—three years younger than Dad—with a loose hold both on a young son and on reality: she would get clean later, but at the time was addicted to speed, ocassionally to the point of psychosis. Once, when Dad tried to take a knife from her, she pulled down, slicing open his hand.

They got married six months after they met, were together 16 years and are still good friends.

“God knows why he stayed with me,” Mum says when she tells me these stories. I only hear them from her.

Dad’s boss made a croquembouche for their wedding. There are photos of Mum standing over it, looking pretty in a gauzy purple dress. The dessert remained intact. Nobody wanted to eat because they were all speeding.

When I tell these stories, to a friend, I say dramatically, “That’s where I come from.”

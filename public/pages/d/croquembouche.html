Croquembouche

I

n 1986, my dad quit his job at a restaurant in Sydney, took a taxi to the airport and tried to catch a flight to New Zealand. They didn’t let him. He’d been in Australia for six months—longer than his visa allowed—so he could board a plane only if it flew him home to France.

He wasn’t ready to go back. He’d been enjoying a prolonged world tour, partly motivated by the desire to put off for as long as possible a mandatory year in the French army.

He went back to the restaurant and asked if he could keep his job. The once and future kitchen hand.

A few months later, working there, he met my mum, a 21-year-old Australian woman, three years younger than him. She wasn’t doing great at the time. She had little contact with a young son (my older brother) and not much of a firmer grasp on reality itself—she’d get clean in a few years, but for most of the ‘80s was addicted to speed, which frequently gave her psychosis. Once, accusing Dad of being in love with a mutual friend, she brandished a kitchen knife. Gently, stupidly, he tried to take it from her. She pulled down, slicing open his hand.

They got married six months after they met and were together 16 years. They’re good friends today.

“I still don’t know why he stayed,” Mum says when she tells me stories from the early days.

Love, I think. And citizenship. (But mostly love.)

“Your father was an angel,” she says. “He saved me.”

For their wedding, Dad’s boss at the restaurant made a croquembouche. I’ve seen the disposable-camera photos of Mum dismantling it. She wore a gauzy purple dress. She looked pretty. But the dessert could have stayed intact—nobody was hungry. Speed kills your appetite, and everyone was speeding.

When I tell these stories to my boyfriend, I say dramatically, “That’s where I come from!” (I think to myself, even more dramatically, “That’s where I’ll always go back to.”)

Now I’m addicted to speed too&emdash;but it’s okay because it’s prescription: little orange circle Adderall pills. I wonder how they stack up.
